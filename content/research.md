My research is driven by the goal of developing principled statistical and machine learning methods to advance our understanding of the human brain and its relationship to cognition and disease. Below are my main research themes.

## Bayesian Methods for Brain Network Analysis

A central theme of my research is developing Bayesian frameworks for modeling brain connectivity networks. I have developed mixed effect models for genetic association studies with brain network phenotypes, as well as longitudinal network regression methods for brain connectome genetics. These methods enable principled inference on how genetic factors shape the brain's structural and functional organization.

## Deep Learning for Dynamic Brain Connectivity

I am developing Bayesian deep learning frameworks for studying dynamic functional connectivity -- how brain network organization changes over time and how these temporal patterns relate to cognitive outcomes. This line of work bridges the flexibility of deep neural networks with the interpretability and uncertainty quantification of Bayesian inference.

## Mediation and Pathway Analysis with Brain Imaging

I develop pathway and mediation analysis methods that incorporate brain network structure as a mediator in studying how risk factors affect clinical outcomes such as disease progression and survival. This work addresses the unique statistical challenges posed by high-dimensional, graph-structured mediators in neuroimaging studies.

## Semiparametric Methods for Complex Biomedical Data

Beyond neuroimaging, I develop Bayesian semiparametric methods for complex data structures arising in clinical and biomedical research, including clustered recurrent events with zero-inflation and terminal events. These methods provide flexible modeling tools for clinical trial and observational study data.
